The Answer

Grounded

During the Middle Formative period (c. 700–500 BCE), Chalcatzingo functioned as a significant centre on the western edge of the Olmec interaction sphere, its authority expressed primarily through monumental rock-cut relief carving rather than large-scale architecture.1 The most-discussed monument, conventionally called Monument 1 or “El Rey,” depicts a seated figure within a stylised cave-mouth from which scrolls and rain motifs emanate — generally read as a ruler positioned at the intersection of political and supernatural authority.23

A series of hillside reliefs (Monuments 2–5) showing processions and figures with elaborate headdresses suggests an elite concerned with the public commemoration of rank and ritual.1 [ BACKGROUND — not retrieved ] In the broader Formative Mesoamerican pattern, such imagery is often associated with hereditary leadership and the legitimation of authority through control of ritual knowledge, though the corpus contains no direct settlement-hierarchy or burial data for Chalcatzingo to confirm this locally.

The retrieved material supports describing Chalcatzingo as a place where authority was displayed and naturalised through iconography; it does not, on its own, establish the internal administrative structure of the site.4
